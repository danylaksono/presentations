Why Simulation, Not Just Optimisation?

For a 'wicked problem', the goal is not to find a single optimal answer, but to explore a range of plausible and defensible futures.

Optimisation

  • Asks: "What is the best solution?"
  • Finds a single, mathematically optimal result.
  • Treats qualitative goals (like "fairness") as rigid constraints.
  • Hides inherent trade-offs inside the model.

Simulation (Our Approach)

  • Asks: "If we do X, what happens?"
  • Allows exploration of multiple "what-if" scenarios.
  • Augments human judgment for complex goals.
  • Makes trade-offs explicit and visible.
Presenter Script (2:15-3:30):
This leads to a critical decision in our methodology: why did we choose simulation over a pure optimisation approach?

Optimisation is powerful, but it's designed to find the single best answer to a well-defined problem. But in public policy, goals like "fairness" or "equity" are difficult to quantify. Trying to do so oversimplifies the problem.

Our simulation-based approach asks a different question: "If we make this decision, what happens?" It doesn't replace the planner's expertise. Instead, it augments it. It allows them to apply their nuanced, qualitative understanding, test different strategies, and see the consequences. Crucially, it makes the trade-offs explicit and visible, which is essential for creating plans that are not just effective, but also transparent and publicly defensible.

The Decarbonisation Planner

Presenter Script (4:00-4:30):
This is the system we built. It consists of four tightly linked views designed to support a seamless, iterative workflow, from identifying target buildings to authoring plan components and instantly seeing their simulated impact on the map.

A Workflow for Policy Exploration

Our system supports an iterative, four-step process (T1-T4):

  • 1. Identify & Characterise Cohorts (T1)
    Use linked map and table to filter and select specific groups of buildings.
  • 2. Author Scenarios via Composition (T2)
    Define and layer modular 'plan components' on the timeline.
  • 3. Compare Spatio-Temporal Outcomes (T3)
    Instantly simulate impacts and visualise them on the glyph-map.
  • 4. Explore Alternatives through Iteration (T4)
    Adjust parameters, re-order components, and immediately see the results.
Presenter Script (4:30-6:00):
The real power comes from the workflow. A planner can identify a cohort, author a specific plan for them, and place it on the timeline. They can then create another component and layer it into the same overall strategy.

The moment a component is created or modified, the map instantly updates with the simulated outcomes. This enables rapid iteration. The planner can ask "what-if" questions and get immediate, visual feedback. This transforms planning from a static process into a dynamic dialogue with the data.

Visualising Complex Trade-offs with Glyphs

Presenter Script (6:00-7:15):
To make the simulation results understandable, we use custom glyphs instead of traditional map layers. The key innovation is the glyph on the right: a mirrored streamgraph embedded directly onto the map.

The timeline reads left-to-right. The green area shows cumulative carbon saved; the red area shows cumulative budget spent. This design allows a planner to see, at a single glance, the efficiency and trajectory of their plan in each area. This direct encoding of the cost-benefit trade-off reduces cognitive burden and allows for much faster, more intuitive comparison.

Managing Scale & Analytical Clarity

Presenter Script (7:15-8:00):
The system handles scale by allowing planners to move between views of individual buildings and aggregated data. To combat the issue where small, dense urban areas can be visually lost, the user can also transform the map into a 'squarified' grid cartogram. This intentionally abstracts geography to give each spatial unit equal visual prominence, turning the map into a powerful analytical canvas for fair comparison.

Anticipated Q&A

Q&A: Glyph Design Rationale

Question: "Your mirrored streamgraph is information-dense. Why not use simpler charts like bars?"

Answer: We optimized for at-a-glance comparative assessment of temporal trade-offs across space.

  1. Direct Comparison: Mirroring 'cost' vs 'carbon saved' forces a direct visual comparison. The shape's symmetry itself becomes a powerful pre-attentive attribute.
  2. Focus on Trajectory: The continuous shape emphasizes the cumulative nature and overall trajectory of the plan.
  3. Space Efficiency at Scale: The smoother shape reduces visual clutter when dozens are displayed on a map.

Q&A: The Use of Cartograms

Question: "You distort geography with the squarified map. Doesn't this risk misleading the user?"

Answer: We are intentionally sacrificing geographic fidelity to gain analytical clarity and perceptual fairness.

  • Standard maps allow large, sparse areas to visually dominate small, dense urban areas.
  • Our grid cartogram gives every spatial unit equal visual prominence, shifting the user's task from *navigation* to *comparison*.
  • The user can always toggle back to the standard geographic view for context.

Q&A: Role of Multi-Objective Decision Making

Question: "How does your system relate to formal Multi-Objective Optimisation (MOO) and the exploration of Pareto fronts?"

MOO & Pareto Fronts

  • Approach: Aims to automatically compute a set of mathematically optimal solutions.
  • Workflow: The user's task is to explore pre-computed results.
  • Challenge: Struggles with qualitative objectives like "social equity."

Our System's Approach

  • Approach: Facilitates human-led exploration of the decision space.
  • Workflow: The user's task is to author scenarios based on their own objectives.
  • Strength: It doesn't automate the decision, it augments the decision-maker.
Presenter Script:
That's a fantastic question. Traditional Multi-Objective Optimisation systems compute a Pareto front of optimal solutions, and the user's task is then to explore those results. This is powerful for well-defined problems.

However, our problem contains messy, qualitative objectives like social equity. So, our system takes a different approach. Instead of automating the decision, we aim to augment the decision-maker. The planner's task is not to explore pre-computed results, but to *author* scenarios themselves.

In essence, the human performs the complex, qualitative weighting of objectives, and our tool serves as an interactive calculator to show them the consequences of their decisions. It's a human-in-the-loop approach to multi-objective decision making.
