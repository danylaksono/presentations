Interactive Visual Analytics for Local Decarbonisation Planning

Empowering Policy-Aligned Scenario Exploration

Dany Laksono, Radu Jianu & Aidan Slingsby

City St. George's, University of London

With the 2050 deadline looming, the UK’s pursuit of its net zero target is a contentious journey marked by legal and technical challenges, the delegation of complex decarbonisation decisions to frontline local authorities, and ongoing debate over the pace and feasibility of the transition.

The Challenge:

Developing effective and equitable decarbonisation plans is a complex balancing act for UK local authorities.

They must balance:

  • Technical factors (Carbon reduction)
  • Economic factors (Costs, budgets)
  • Social factors (Fuel poverty, equity)

…exacerbated by:

  • Conflicting stakeholder priorities.
  • Massive, multivariate datasets.
  • No single ‘optimal’ solution.

This is a wicked problem in decision making.

Current approaches: An optimisation-based model for decarbonisation planning to support Local Authority decision-making:

We identified some limitations with this approach.

First, the monolithic optimisation models hide the trade-offs inherent in the planning process.

  • it is slow. The model can take hours (or even a day) to run.
  • it is not transparent to the user. The user cannot see the trade-offs inherent in the planning process.
  • it often fails to model the complex interactions between different factors in real-world scenarios.

Second, the choropleth map layer metaphor increases cognitive load by forcing users to mentally integrate fragmented information.

  • it is complicated to interpret the results of the model.
  • it is not capturing the nuances of multi-scale decarbonisation data.
  • temporal dynamics are not well represented in the choropleth map.
  • moreover, comparison between multiple administrative areas is difficult.

Our Approach: A Human-in-the-Loop Paradigm

We ask a different question: “If we make this decision, what happens?”.

  1. Modular, Component-Based Planning
    Decomposes complex strategy-building into manageable, interpretable parts.
  2. Multi-Scale, Glyph-Based Visualisation
    Represents multivariate and temporal data uniformly across geographic scales.
  3. An Integrated, Iterative Workflow
    Tightly couples data exploration, simulation, and outcome analysis for real-time feedback.

By enabling an exploratory process with visual analytics, it augments expert judgment and makes trade-offs explicit.

A Workflow for Policy Exploration

Our system supports an iterative, four-step process (T1-T4):

  1. Identify & Characterise Cohorts (T1)
    Use linked map and table to filter and select groups of buildings.
  2. Author Scenarios via Composition (T2)
    Define and layer modular plan components on the timeline.
  3. Compare Spatio-Temporal Outcomes (T3)
    Instantly simulate impacts and visualise them on the glyph-map.
  4. Explore Alternatives through Iteration (T4)
    Adjust parameters, re-order components, and immediately see the results.

T1: Identify Cohorts

Use the linked Table and Map views to select specific groups of buildings for intervention

T2: Author Scenarios

Compose a strategy from modular “plan components” based on the available list of green technologies, targetted at the selected buildings.

T3: Simulate and Evaluate

Instantly view the multivariate data and spatio-temporal outcomes of the plan represented as glyphs across both the map and the table.

T4: Iterate and Refine

Modify plan composition, learn from immediate visual feedback, and adjust the strategy in a dynamic dialogue with the data.

Visualising Complex Trade-offs with Glyphs

The glyph design replaces layered cartograms to visualise multivariate data.

Conclusion & Future Work

We reframed decarbonisation planning from a rigid, monolithic process into a transparent, iterative dialogue informed by contextual interviews with the industrial partner and client.

Future work will focus on:

  1. LLM-based user interface – developing a natural language interface that enables decision-makers to interact with complex models more intuitively.
  2. Further user studies – conducting structured evaluations with local authority planners to assess usability, trust, and decision support value.
  3. Side-by-side scenario comparison – enabling direct visual and quantitative comparison of alternative decarbonisation pathways to support informed trade-offs.
  4. Scalability and generalisation – extending the framework to other local authorities and contexts beyond the initial case study.
